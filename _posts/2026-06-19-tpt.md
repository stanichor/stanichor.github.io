[Tailcalled created a new personality test](https://tailcalled.substack.com/p/which-personality-traits-are-real), the [Targeted Personality Test](https://www.onlinetests.me/test/pers0/). One of its purposes was to investigate the "realness" of personality traits without relying on the lexical hypothesis. This is done by asking people about concrete, narrow behaviors instead of abstract adjectives. I thought I'd also have a go at the data, both for robustness reasons and because it's fun.

In their post, Tailcalled looks at three measures of how well a trait performs:
- **Trait impact**: The average loading of the concrete behaviors on the trait, calculated by taking the square root of the average correlation of the concrete behaviors with each other. (This measures how much the trait impacts behavior.)
- **Factor model loss**: The difference between the observed trait correlation matrix and a hypothetical correlation matrix if there were only a single factor. (This measures whether multiple traits are being conflated.)
- **Correlation with lexical notion**: I'm not quite sure how this is calculated, other than that it's based on the correlations between the abstract items and concrete items after adjusting for the fact that the concrete items do not perfectly correlate among themselves.

I thought I'd also have a go with my own versions of these measures. For factor model loss, I perform [parallel analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_analysis) on the concrete items for a trait and report the number of factors it suggests. For the other two measures, I fit a two-factor model in which the concrete items load on one factor and the abstract items load on a second factor, with the two factors allowed to correlate. For trait impact, I report the [average variance extracted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_variance_extracted) (AVE) of the concrete factor (you can determine the "equivalent" loading by taking the square root). For correlation with lexical notion, I use the correlation between the concrete and abstract factors.

## Factor Model Loss

To measure factor model loss, I performed parallel analysis on the concrete items for a trait and report the number of factors it suggested. Traits were one factor was suggested should be those where the concrete items reflect a single underlying construct. Traits with multiple factors may possibly be conflating several distinct constructs under the same label.

| Factors | Traits |
| ------- | ------- | 
| 1       | Art Appreciation, Conservatism, Honesty, Sociability, Well-Being |
| 2       | Adaptability, Anxiety, Attention-Seeking, Authoritarianism, Charisma, Compassion, Creativity, Emotional Expressiveness, Emotional Stability, Humor, Impulsivity, Industriousness, Intellect, Introspection, Irritability, Orderliness, Perfectionism, Self-Control, Sensation-Seeking |
| 3       | Conformity, Easy-Goingness, Trust |

## Correlation with Lexical Notion + Trait Impact

For each trait, I fit a two-factor model in which the concrete items load on one factor and the abstract items load on a second factor. 

The number in parentheses beside the trait name is the correlation between the concrete and abstract factors. Higher values indicate that the abstract trait label corresponds more closely to the cluster of concrete behaviors.

Within each trait, I also report √AVE (the square root of the average variance extracted) for both factors. This serves as a measure of trait impact: higher values indicate that the factor explains more of the variation in its items.

## Implications

While reassigning the items makes the factors clearer, it also raises concerns about personality tests in general. According to Tailcalled, the concrete items were derived by first giving the [SPI-81-27&5](https://www.sapa-project.org/research/SPI/SPIforms/SPI_81_27_&_5_v1_1.pdf) test to people and then asking those who scored high or low on various traits to describe examples of how they could be said to possess (or not possess) those traits. Those responses were then turned into a second personality test consisting of more concrete behaviors.

Given this, the fact that so many items do not load on their assigned factors is concerning. The reason measuring personality is useful is that it lets us predict behavior. If there are no behaviors that can be predicted by a personality trait, why does the trait matter at all?

A reasonable assumption is that when someone responds to an abstract item (such as "I appreciate art"), they think about whether they exhibit specific behaviors, such as visiting art galleries, associating art with snobbery, or creating decorations. Depending on how strongly they exhibit such behaviors, they decide whether they agree or disagree with the statement.

For a trait like Art Appreciation, this process seems to work well. You ask people who agree with "I appreciate art" what exactly they mean, and they give answers like visiting art galleries or enjoying looking at art. When we look at responses to the concrete-item test, those behaviors correlate strongly with one another and with the corresponding abstract items. People who visit art galleries also tend to enjoy looking at art, which is good and expected.

But for a trait like Easy-Goingness, something appears to have gone wrong. People who score highly on Easy-Goingness (where an abstract item might look like "I take it easy") give examples such as not spending much effort on parenting, not getting angry about politics, or watching TV in the evenings. Yet these behaviors do not seem to correlate very well with one another. This implies that when people think about a statement such as "I take it easy", they may all be thinking about different, unrelated things.

In this sense, traits like Art Appreciation, (Political) Conservatism, or Charisma seem real: when asked about them, people appear to have roughly the same idea of what they mean. Traits like Easy-Goingness, Adaptability, or Conformity seem much less real: everyone has a different idea of what they mean. More work to determine which personality traits are real, and to better reify the less real ones, seems warranted.

[^reassignment]: To reassign the items, I fit a model in which items load on all factors. The item loadings have a spike-and-slab prior, and I count an item as assigned to a factor if its posterior loading on that factor has an absolute value greater than or equal to 0.10.
